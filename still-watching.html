Why we forget — and what we can do about it

When Alan Kurdi's body washed up on the shores of Turkey, the world was set alight. Global media brought the immediacy of the crisis onto screens worldwide. As a million refugees arrived that year to Greece's islands, volunteer groups and humanitarian relief flooded in.

Ten years on, most people think things are better. They're not. Sea arrivals have risen to over 40,000 a year — well above 2017-2018 levels. Pushbacks — the forcible and illegal practice of dragging asylum-seeking boats back out of national waters — is commonplace. Funding for non-profits has collapsed. Asylum-seekers live in high-security facilities in the remotest reaches of the islands. Out of sight, out of mind.

"It's human for us to look for the happy ending. To imagine that things generally get better over time. Human, but not helpful."

The problem isn't just that we have a limited attention span — it's that our attention is a limited moral resource too. We can only care about a certain number of things. Covid-19 brought that attention closer to home. The invasions of Ukraine and Gaza have pulled it elsewhere again.

The gap in how we consume news

A train crashes. A building collapses. Floodwaters rise. We talk about it for a few days, and then we move on. We never learn what the conclusion was, what the official response was, or what the results of any investigation might have been. The story gets an opening act, but never a resolution.

Still Watching is a small tool to help fix that. You pick a handful of stories that genuinely matter to you, set a check-in date, and we remind you to go looking for the follow-up. Not a flood of notifications — just a quiet prompt, at an interval you choose, to ask: are things actually better now?

How to use it

Go to My stories and add one or two things you've been following — or want to follow. Choose whether you'd like to be prompted with search queries, or shown links to current news coverage. Then come back when it's time.

The Browse stories tab has a curated list of ongoing situations where the follow-up is usually absent from the news cycle. You can add any of them to your watchlist with one click.
